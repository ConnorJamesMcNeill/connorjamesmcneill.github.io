### I bought two 2007 Honda CRF450Rs for $4000 as a package deal, knowing one of them had a blown engine. The idea was straightforward: fix or sell the good one to fund rebuilding the bad one. What I hadn't accounted for was how much the seller's disassembly would complicate things. The failure bike came with a garbage bag of unsorted parts, an engine that had punched a hole through the crankcase, and no clear record of what had been pulled apart in what order. The good bike needed sorting too, which is where I started.

### Bike 1 turned out to have a charging problem traced to a failed stator. Diagnosing it meant pulling the left side cover and testing the stator output, which pointed clearly at the stator itself. I sourced a replacement, installed it, and had the bike running. Then, partly out of curiosity and partly because I was already in there, I tore into the clutch. That was unnecessary. The clutch was fine, the teardown accomplished nothing, and reassembling it cost time I didn't need to spend. Lesson noted. Bike 1 sold, proceeds went toward Bike 2.

### The failure bike's case damage took months to trace properly. A hole punched through the crankcase near the main bearing is a dramatic failure mode, and the root cause wasn't immediately obvious. After working through the evidence, the most likely explanation was oil starvation leading to main bearing failure, which drove the connecting rod into the case. The rebuild meant a new crankcase, new piston and rod, new oil pump shaft and drive gear, new Hinson clutch basket, and a full set of seals and gaskets. Every wear item got replaced while it was apart. By the end of the summer the bike was running and the project was closed out, though the final accounting came out about a thousand dollars in the red. Most of that was tuition for understanding how a high-revving single-cylinder four-stroke comes apart and goes back together. It turned out to be directly relevant the following year when I joined the FSAE team.
